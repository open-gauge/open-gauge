---
title: Drift metrics
description: How Open Gauge compares two calibration curves — max/mean/RMS drift, offset, gain, and residual drift.
---

To measure how much a sensor has drifted, Open Gauge compares two of its calibration curves — a
reference calibration (typically the baseline/first one) and a current one — by evaluating
both fitted polynomials across their **shared operating range** and looking at the difference
curve between them, $d(x) = f_{\text{current}}(x) - f_{\text{reference}}(x)$.

## Max drift [#max-drift]

The largest absolute difference between the reference and current curves across the shared
operating range:

$$
\text{max drift} = \max_x \left| d(x) \right|
$$

The single biggest weight in the [health score](./overview.mdx#score)
(30%) — it answers "at its worst point, how far has this sensor's calibration moved?"

## Mean drift [#mean-drift]

The average absolute difference between the two curves across the shared range:

$$
\text{mean drift} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \left| d(x_i) \right|
$$

## RMS drift [#rms-drift]

The root-mean-square difference between the two curves — penalizes larger deviations more
heavily than mean drift does, the same way [RMSE](../calibration/curve-fitting.mdx#rmse)
penalizes large residuals more than a simple average would:

$$
\text{RMS drift} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n d(x_i)^2}
$$

## Offset [#offset]

The difference between the curves at the *start* of the shared range — a constant (zero-point)
shift. A sensor that has drifted by a roughly constant amount across its whole range, rather
than growing worse toward one end, shows up mostly as offset.

## Gain [#gain]

The slope of the difference curve $d(x)$ — a change in *sensitivity* (span) between the two
calibrations, as opposed to a constant offset. A sensor whose span has stretched or compressed
(e.g. from a reference-junction or amplifier gain shift) shows up mostly as gain, not offset.

## Residual drift [#residual-drift]

The difference between the curves at the *end* of the range minus the difference at the
*start* — i.e. drift that isn't explained by a constant offset alone. Combined with
[gain](#gain), this separates "the whole curve shifted uniformly" from "the curve tilted."
